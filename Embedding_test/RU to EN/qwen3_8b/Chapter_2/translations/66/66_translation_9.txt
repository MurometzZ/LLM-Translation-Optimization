The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a restless and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and gazed into the distance for a long time and intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he was going to the university, it usually happened—more often than not, when returning home—that he would stop exactly at this same spot, gaze intently at this truly magnificent panorama, and each time almost marvel at one obscure and unsolvable impression of his own. An inexplicable coldness always seemed to waft over him from this magnificent panorama; this rich picture was full of a silent and deaf spirit… He was always fascinated by his somber and enigmatic impression and postponed solving it, distrusting himself, to the future. Now suddenly he recalled these past questions and uncertainties of his, and it seemed to him that he had not accidentally recalled them now. It seemed strange and strange to him that he had stopped at the same spot again, as before, as if he had indeed imagined that he could think about the same thing now as before, and be interested in the same themes and pictures as before… just so recently. It almost seemed funny to him at the same time, and yet it pressed his chest painfully. In some depth, below, somewhere barely visible under his feet, it seemed to him now all this past, these old thoughts, these old tasks, these old themes, these old impressions, and this entire panorama, and himself, and everything, everything… It seemed as if he was flying somewhere upward and everything was vanishing from his eyes… Making an involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a double-rouble coin clenched in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung his arm, and threw it into the water; then he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had somehow cut himself off from everything and everyone at this moment with scissors.
