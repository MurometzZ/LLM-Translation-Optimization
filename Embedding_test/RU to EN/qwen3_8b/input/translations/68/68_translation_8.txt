And lo and behold, to the greatest astonishment, he suddenly recognized his mistress's voice. She was howling, screaming, and wailing, rushing, hurrying, spilling words so fast that it was impossible to make out what she was saying, pleading—obviously, begging them to stop beating her, because they were unmercifully beating her on the stairs. The voice of the attacker had become so monstrous from hatred and fury that it was barely more than a growl, yet the attacker himself was also saying something, speaking hurriedly, incoherently, rushing and gasping. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf: he recognized this voice; it was Ily Petrovich's voice. Ily Petrovich was here, beating his mistress! He was kicking her with his feet, hitting her head against the steps—this was clear, one could hear it by the sounds, the screams, the blows! What was this, had the world turned upside down? One could hear a crowd gathering on all the floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, doors slamming, footsteps, and people gathering. "But why, why, and how could this be!"—he kept repeating, seriously thinking he had gone mad. But no, he was hearing it too clearly...
