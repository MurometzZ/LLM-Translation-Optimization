He remembered only that he had taken a single sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came amnesia. III

He, however, was not entirely in a state of amnesia throughout his illness; it was a feverish condition, with delirium and semi-consciousness. Later, he recalled many things. At times, it seemed to him that a crowd had gathered around him, wanting to take him somewhere, and there was much argument and quarreling about him. At other times, he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone having left and fearing him, opening the door only occasionally and slightly to look at him, threatening him, whispering to each other about something, laughing, and mocking him. He often remembered Nastasya by his side; he distinguished another person, very familiar to him, but could not recall who it was, and he longed for this, even crying. Sometimes it seemed to him that he had been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about that he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something he could not forget—tormenting himself, suffering, recalling, groaning, falling into rage or a terrible, unbearable fear. Then he would try to get up, wanting to run away, but someone would always stop him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and amnesia.
