—"And a cup of tea?" "A cup of tea, if you like." "Pour it out. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately gave orders, poured some out, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast and sat back on the divan again. He still held the sick man's head in his left hand, lifted him up and began to feed him tea from a teaspoon, blowing continuously and especially energetically on the spoon as if this process of blowing was the main and most vital point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite enough strength to lift himself up and sit on the divan without any external help, and not only hold his hands well enough to grasp a spoon or cup, but even walk. But for some strange, almost bestial cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for the time being, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even not quite understanding, and in the meantime listen and find out what is happening here? However, he could not overcome his aversion: after taking ten spoons of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away and fell back onto the pillow.
