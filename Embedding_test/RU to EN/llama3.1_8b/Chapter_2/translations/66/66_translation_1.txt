The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; one anxious and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked far off for a long time, intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to university, it often happened that, returning home, he would stop exactly at this same spot, gaze intently at this indeed magnificent panorama, and almost every time be amazed by one obscure and insoluble impression of his own. An inexplicable chill always blew on him from this magnificent panorama; for him the spirit was mute and deaf to this lush picture... He marveled each time at his own gloomy and enigmatic impression and put off solving it, not trusting himself, until later. Suddenly he remembered now these old questions and doubts of his, and it seemed to him that he had not forgotten them by chance. It struck him as strange and wonderful that he had stopped in the same place as before, as if he really imagined that he could think about the same things now as before, with the same themes and pictures that interested him... still so recently. Even a little bit funny it seemed to him, and at the same time his chest constricted with pain. In some depth, below, somewhere just visible underfoot, it seemed to him now that all this past was, and old thoughts, and old problems, and old themes, and old impressions, and the whole panorama, and he himself, and everything... It seemed as if he was flying upwards somewhere and everything disappeared from his eyes. Having made one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a double ruble coin clenched in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung it back and forth, and threw it into the water; then turned around and went home. It seemed to him that he had cut himself off from everything and everyone with scissors just now.
