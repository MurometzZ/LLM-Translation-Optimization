Further in the courtyard, peering out from behind a fence was the corner of a low, soot-covered stone shed, obviously part of some workshop. Here, certainly, there was some establishment, a carriage or locksmith's shop, or something like that; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust swirled. "I wish I could just throw it somewhere and leave!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he walked through the gates and just saw, right next to the gates, a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, cab drivers, etc.), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written with chalk the usual sharpness in such cases: "Here trespassing is forbidden." So it's good that he didn't arouse any suspicion that he had gone in and stopped. "Everything here is so convenient to just throw somewhere into a heap and leave!"

Glancing around once more, he already stuck his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where all the distance was only as wide as an arshin, he noticed a large uncut stone, weighing about one and a half poods, lying flat against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk; you could hear passersby shuffling by, of which there are always many here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone from the street happened to come in, which, however, was quite possible, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and turned over the stone. Under the stone a small depression formed; immediately he began throwing everything from his pocket into it.
