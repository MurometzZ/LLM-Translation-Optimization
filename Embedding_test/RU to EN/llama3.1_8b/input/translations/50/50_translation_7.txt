Further in the courtyard, peering out from behind a fence was an angle of a low, soot-stained stone shed, obviously part of some workshop. Here, certainly, there had been some establishment, a carriage or locksmith's shop, or something like that; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust was scattered. "Just think where to throw it and leave!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he walked through the gates and just saw, right next to the gates, a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses with many factory workers, artisans, cabmen, etc.) attached to the fence, and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharpness in such cases: "Here trespassing is forbidden." So it's good that there's no suspicion that he went in and stopped. "Everything here is so quickly gathered up and thrown somewhere into a heap and leave!"

Glancing around once more, he had already stuffed his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where all the distance was only as wide as an arshin, he noticed a large uncut stone, weighing about one and a half poods, lying flat against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk, you could hear pedestrians shuffling by, of which there are always many here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone from the street came in, which, however, could very well happen, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength and turned over the stone. Under the stone a small depression formed; immediately he began throwing everything from his pocket into it.
