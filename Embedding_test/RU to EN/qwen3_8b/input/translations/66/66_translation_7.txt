The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked into the distance for a long time, gazing intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he walked to the university, he usually, most often on his way home, found himself stopping exactly at this same spot, staring intently at this truly magnificent panorama and was almost surprised each time by a single unclear and unsolvable impression of his own. An unexplainable coldness always seemed to emanate from this magnificent panorama; this rich picture was full of a silent and deaf spirit… He marveled each time at his gloomy and enigmatic impression and postponed solving it, doubting himself, to the future. Now suddenly he sharply recalled these former questions and doubts of his, and it seemed to him that he had not accidentally recalled them now. It seemed strange and strange to him that he had stopped at the same spot again, as before, as if he had indeed imagined that he could think about the same things now as before, and be interested in the same themes and pictures as before… just so recently. It almost seemed funny to him at the same time, and yet it pressed painfully on his chest. In some depth, below, somewhere barely visible under his feet, it seemed to him now all this former past, and former thoughts, and former tasks, and former themes, and former impressions, and the entire panorama, and himself, and everything, everything… It seemed as if he was flying away somewhere upwards and everything was vanishing from his eyes… Making one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a two-kopeck coin clenched in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung his arm, and threw it into the water; then he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had cut himself off from all and everything at this very moment with scissors.
