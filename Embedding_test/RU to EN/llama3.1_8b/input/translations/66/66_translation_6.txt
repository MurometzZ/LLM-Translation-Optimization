The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; one anxious and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked far off for a long time, intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to university, it often happened that, returning home, he would stop at exactly this same spot, gaze intently at this indeed magnificent panorama, and almost every time be amazed by one obscure and insoluble impression of his own. An inexplicable chill always blew over him from this magnificent panorama; the spirit was mute and deaf to him in this lush picture... He marveled each time at his gloomy and enigmatic impression and put off solving it, not trusting himself, for the future. Suddenly he remembered now all these former questions and perplexities of his, and it seemed to him that he hadn't recalled them by chance. One thing struck him as strange and wonderful: that he had stopped at exactly the same spot as before, as if he really believed that he could think about the very same things now as before, and be interested in the very same old themes and pictures as he was... just a little while ago. It even made him smile slightly, but at the same time it squeezed his chest with pain. In some depth, below, somewhere barely visible underfoot, everything from his past seemed to appear to him now: all his former thoughts, problems, topics of interest, and impressions, and this entire panorama, and he himself, and everything... It seemed that he was flying upwards somehow, and everything disappeared from his eyes. Having made one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a two-kopeck piece clenched in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung it back and forth, and then threw it into the water; after that, he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had cut himself off from everything and everyone with scissors just now.
