—"A cup of tea?" — "Let me have a cup of tea." — "Pour it." "Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit at the table." He immediately arranged, poured, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back on the couch. He continued to support the patient's head with his left hand, lifted him, and began to feed him with a teaspoon, blowing continuously and especially diligently on the spoon, as if the act of blowing was the main and saving point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling he had sufficient strength to lift himself up and sit on the couch without any outside help, and not only to control his hands enough to hold the spoon or cup, but even perhaps to walk. But due to some strange, almost animal cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even as if not yet fully understanding, while at the same time listening and finding out what was going on here? Nevertheless, he could not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed away the spoon, and collapsed again onto the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows under his head—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.
