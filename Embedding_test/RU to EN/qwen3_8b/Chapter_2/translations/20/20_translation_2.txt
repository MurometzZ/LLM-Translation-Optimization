It was some particularly disheveled person with an unmoving idea in his gaze. "You won't learn anything from this, because it's all the same to him," Raskolnikov thought. "Go there, to the scribe," the clerk said, and pushed forward with his finger, indicating the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), a cramped and densely packed space filled with people, somewhat better dressed than in those rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One, in mourning attire, poorly dressed, sat at the table opposite the scribe and was writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and flushed red with spots, an obvious woman, dressed extremely elaborately, with a brooch on her chest the size of a tea saucer, stood aside and was waiting for something. Raskolnikov handed his summons to the scribe. The scribe glanced at it briefly, said, "Wait," and continued working with the mourning lady. He breathed more freely.
