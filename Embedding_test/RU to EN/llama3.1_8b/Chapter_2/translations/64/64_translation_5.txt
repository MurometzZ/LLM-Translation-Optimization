But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolsky Bridge he was forced to wake up again completely due to one rather unpleasant incident for him. A coachman of a carriage struck him sharply with his whip on the back, because he almost got under the horses' hooves, despite the fact that the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that he, jumping away to the railing (he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where they drive, not walk), maliciously clicked his teeth and hissed through them. Of course, laughter echoed all around: "And what's the matter?!" - "Some beggar." - "It's known, a drunkard will come up to you on purpose and run under the wheels; and you'll be responsible for him." - "They make their living that way, sir, they make their living..." But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still looked after the departing carriage with senseless and malicious glances, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was shoving money into his hands.
