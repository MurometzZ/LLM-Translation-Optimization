But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still mindlessly and spitefully looked after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shove money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman in a headscarf and goat-skin boots, and with her a young girl in a hat and with a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. It was a two-kopeck coin. By their dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real street collector, and the act of giving a whole two-kopeck coin probably deserved a whip, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two-kopeck coin in his hand, walked ten steps, and then turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is very rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be seen better from any point than from here, from the bridge, twenty steps away from the chapel, shone brilliantly, and through the clear air, one could clearly see even every ornament on it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the blow; now only a restless and somewhat unclear thought occupied him entirely.
