But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolsky Bridge he was forced to wake up once again due to a rather unpleasant incident for him. A coachman from one of the carriages gave him a tight lash on his back with his whip because he almost got under the horses' hooves, despite the fact that the coachman had yelled at him three or four times. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that he, jumping away to the railing (he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where they drive, not walk), snarled and snapped his teeth with malice. Of course, laughter echoed all around: "And what's the matter?!" "Some beggar." "It's known, a drunkard will come up on purpose and run under the wheels; and you'll be responsible for him." "They make their living that way, sir, they make their living..." But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still looked after the departing carriage with a senseless and malicious gaze, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slipping some money into his hands.
