Destined by nature to be the "lords of fate," they boldly rose up against the existing order while defiantly violating widely recognized moral norms, not hesitating before violence and crime in order to impose their will upon humanity. Condemned by their contemporaries, these "extraordinary people" were later justified by posterity, which recognized them as heroes of history and benefactors of mankind. From this individualistic, anarchic system of ideas, a system he not only thought through in solitude but also outlined in a newspaper article a year before committing the murder, arises the dilemma that Raskolnikov formulates in words: "Am I a mere worm like all others, or a man?" "Am I a trembling creature, or do I have a right?" 

The hero of "Crime and Punishment" — and this is one of the reasons for his tragic misunderstanding — does not seriously consider why thousands and millions of workers in past epochs and in his time were not capable of historical creation, could not actively protest and struggle against unjust living conditions. His consciousness of their past and present submissiveness provokes both anger and sorrow in him, yet at the same time impels him to elevate himself in his own eyes to an immeasurable height, to oppose himself to the masses, the people, the "ordinary," common individuals. Raskolnikov does not wish — unlike most of the people he observes — to silently obey and endure, and this moral intransigence of the hero impresses both the author and the reader of the novel. But from this, according to Raskolnikov's view, only one conclusion is possible — he must prove to himself and to others that, like other "extraordinary" historical figures, he is not "a trembling creature," but a naturally born "lord of fate," having the right to transgress elementary moral laws and norms, recognized as unbreakable by ordinary, common people. This conclusion leads Raskollynkov to his crime, which he considers a test necessary to determine whether he belongs to the race of "extraordinary" people or whether he remains to obey and endure, like others, ordinary weak natures. By committing the crime, Raskolnikov challenges the world of social inequality and the suppression of human individuality. At the same time (although the hero of Dostoevsky is not aware of this), his "idea" immortalizes the inhumanity of the existing order of things. The inequality between the strong and the weak, between predators and the oppressed, forming the basis of class society and the state, remains for Raskolnikov in his reflections an eternal model of any human coexistence.
