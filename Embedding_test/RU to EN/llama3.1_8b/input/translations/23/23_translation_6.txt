The plump lady jumped up from her seat at the sight of him and with a peculiar excitement began to squat; but the officer paid no attention to her whatsoever, and she was no longer able to sit next to him. This was a lieutenant, an assistant to the quarter-master sergeant, with horizontally protruding reddish sideburns and extremely small facial features that expressed nothing in particular except for some haughtiness. He glanced at Raskolnikov from the corner of his eye and partly with indignation: Raskolnikov's suit was too shabby, and despite all the humiliation, he still didn't have a proper posture; Raskolnikov looked at him carelessly, too directly and for too long, so that the lieutenant even took offense. — What do you want? — he shouted, probably surprised that such a ragamuffin didn't even think to bow down from his lightning-fast glance. — They demanded... by summons... — Raskolnikov replied somehow awkwardly. — This is about collecting money from them, from the student, — the clerk hastened, tearing himself away from the papers. — Here! — and he tossed a notebook at Raskolnikov, pointing to a place in it, — read!
