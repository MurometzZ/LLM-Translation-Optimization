The clerk began dictating to him the usual form of such a letter, namely that he could not pay, promising to do so someday, that he would not leave the city, and that he would neither sell nor give away his property, etc. — "You can't write," said the clerk, looking at Raskolnikov with curiosity as his pen slipped from his fingers. "Are you ill?" — "Yes... my head is spinning..." — "Go on; sign it." The clerk took back the paper and attended to other matters. Raskolnikov handed over the pen but instead of standing up and leaving, he placed both elbows on the table and clasped his hands around his head. It was as if they were hammering a nail into his forehead. A strange idea suddenly came to him: to stand up now, approach Nikodim Fyodorovich and tell him everything that had happened yesterday, down to the last detail, then go with them to the apartment and point out the things in the corner, in the hole. The urge was so strong that he already stood up from his seat, ready to carry it out.
