— Well, isn't she my mistress? — So what if she is your mistress? The clerk looked at him with a condescending smile of sorrow, mixed with a certain triumph, as if looking at a new recruit just beginning to be fired upon: "So, what do you feel now, my dear?" But what did it matter now to him about borrowed writing, about the claim! Was it worth even the slightest worry, let alone any attention! He stood, read, listened, answered, even asked questions himself, but all this was mechanical. The triumph of self-preservation, salvation from the crushing danger—this filled his entire being at that moment, without forethought, without analysis, without future speculations or guesses, without doubts or questions. It was the moment of complete, immediate, purely animal joy. But at that very moment, in the office, something like thunder and lightning occurred. The lieutenant, still shaken by the disrespect, burning with anger, obviously wanting to support the wounded ambition, launched himself at the unfortunate "plump lady," who had been looking at him with the most foolish smile since he had entered. — You, such a silly and peculiar one, — he suddenly shouted at the top of his lungs (the mourning lady had already left), — what happened during the past night?
