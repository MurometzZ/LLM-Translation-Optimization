—"Some tea?" — "Sure, some tea." — "Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit at the table." He immediately arranged, poured, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back on the sofa. He still wrapped his left hand around the patient's head, lifted him up, and began feeding him with a teaspoon of tea, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the main and saving point of recovery lay in this blowing process. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling he had quite enough strength to lift himself up and sit on the sofa without any outside help, and not only to use his hands enough to hold the spoon or cup, but even perhaps to walk. But by some strange, almost beast-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to hide, to pretend, if necessary, even as if not yet fully understanding, while at the same time listening and finding out what was going on here? In any case, he could not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, poutfully pushed away the spoon, and collapsed back onto the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows under his head—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.
