But at that moment, as he stood by the railings and still senselessly and spitefully looked after the receding carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shove money into his hands. He looked around: an old widow in a headscarf and wooden clogs, and with her a girl in a hat and with a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. Two kopecks. By their dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real street collector, and perhaps he was due a whip's strike for the offering, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two kopecks in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which rarely happens on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be seen better from any point than from here, from the bridge, twenty steps away from the chapel, shone brilliantly, and through the clear air, one could clearly see even every ornament of it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the strike; a restless and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively.
