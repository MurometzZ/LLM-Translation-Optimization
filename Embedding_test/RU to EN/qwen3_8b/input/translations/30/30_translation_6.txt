—Ilya Petrovich!— the scribe began, solicitously, but stopped to wait, since there was no way to hold the boiling corporal except by the hands, which he knew from his own experience. As for the dignified lady, at first she trembled from thunder and lightning; but strange thing: the more numerous and stronger the curses became, the more amiable her demeanor grew, and the more charming her smile became, directed toward the furious corporal. She stepped about in place and kept bowing, impatiently waiting for her turn to speak, and she waited until she was allowed to say her piece.—There's no noise or scuffle here, sir captain—she suddenly burst out, as if spilling peas, with a strong German accent, though speaking Russian fluently—no, no scuffle at all, and they came drunk, and I'll tell you everything, sir captain, and I'm not to blame… I have a noble home, sir captain, and noble manners, sir captain, and I've always, always wanted no scuffle. And they came completely drunk, and then they asked for three drinks, and then one raised his legs and started playing the piano with his foot, and that's very bad in a noble home, and he broke the piano, and there's absolutely no manners here, and I said so. And he took a stick and started pushing everyone from behind with it. And then I quickly called the stable boy and Karl came, he took Karl and hit him in the eye, and Henrietta also got hit in the eye, and I got hit five times on the cheek. And that's very indecent in a noble home, sir captain, and I shouted. And he opened the window and started screaming like a little pig from the window; and that's a disgrace.
