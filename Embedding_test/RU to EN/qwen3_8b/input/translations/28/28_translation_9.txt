— Well, isn't she my mistress? — So what if she is your mistress? The scribe looked at him with a compassionate, condescending smile, mixed with a certain triumph, as if he were a novice just beginning to be bombarded: "So, what do you feel now, eh?" But what did it matter now to him about borrowed writing, about reprimands! Was it worth even the slightest worry, let alone any attention at all! He stood, read, listened, answered, even asked questions himself, but all this was mechanical. The triumph of self-preservation, salvation from the pressing danger—this filled his entire being at that moment, without forethought, without analysis, without future speculations or guesses, without doubts or questions. It was a moment of complete, immediate, purely animal joy. But at that very moment, in the office, something like thunder and lightning occurred. The ensign, still shaken by the disrespect, burning with anger, obviously wanting to support the wounded ambition, launched himself at the unfortunate "puffy lady," who had been looking at him since he entered with the most idiotic smile. — You, such and such and so and so, — he suddenly shouted at the top of his lungs (the mourning lady had already left), — what happened during the past night?
