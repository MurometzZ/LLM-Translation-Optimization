—"A cup of tea?" — "I'll have a cup of tea." — "Pour it." — "Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit at the table." He got up immediately, poured the tea, then poured another cup, dropped his breakfast, and sat back on the couch. He continued to hold the patient's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began to feed him with a teaspoon, pouring tea into it, again continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the entire main and saving point of recovery lay in this act of blowing. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite strong enough to lift himself up and sit on the couch without any outside help, and not only to control his hands enough to hold the spoon or cup, but even perhaps to walk. But for some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even though he didn't quite understand yet, but at the same time to listen and find out what was going on here? In any case, he could not overcome his revulsion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away, and collapsed back onto the pillow. Under his head, indeed, there were now real down-filled pillows with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.
