He remembered only that he had taken a sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came amnesia. III

He, however, was not entirely in a state of amnesia throughout his illness; it was a feverish condition, with delirium and semi-consciousness. Much later he recalled many things. At times it seemed to him that a crowd had gathered around him and wanted to take him away somewhere, and there was much arguing and quarreling about him. At other times he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone having left and fearing him, opening the door only occasionally to look at him, threatening him, whispering to each other about something, laughing and mocking him. He often remembered Nastasya by his side; he distinguished another person, who seemed very familiar to him, but could not recall who it was, and he longed for this, even crying. Sometimes it seemed to him that he had been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about everything—he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something he could not forget—tormented, suffering, recalling, groaning, falling into rage or into a terrible, unbearable fear. Then he would try to get up and run away, but someone would always stop him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and confusion.
