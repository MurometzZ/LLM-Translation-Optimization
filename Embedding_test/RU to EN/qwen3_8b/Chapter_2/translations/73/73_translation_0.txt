He remembered only that he had taken a single sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came amnesia. III

He, however, was not entirely in a state of amnesia throughout the illness; it was a feverish condition, with delirium and semi-consciousness. Much later he recalled many things. At times it seemed to him that a crowd had gathered around him and wanted to take him away somewhere, and there was much arguing and quarreling about him. At other times he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone had left, and they feared him; they only occasionally opened the door slightly to look at him, threatened him, whispered among themselves about something, laughed, and teased him. He often remembered Nastasya by his side; he distinguished also another person, very much like someone he knew, but he could not figure out who it was and pined for this, even crying. Sometimes it seemed to him that he had already been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about that he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something he could not forget—tormented himself, suffered, recalling, groaned, fell into a frenzy or an unbearable terror. Then he would try to get up, wanting to run away, but someone always stopped him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and amnesia.
