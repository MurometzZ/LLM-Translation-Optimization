But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolayevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. A coachman of one carriage struck him hard on the back with a whip for barely avoiding being run over by the horses, despite the coachman having shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), growled fiercely, and gnashed his teeth. Laughter echoed around him. — "To work!" — someone said. "Some drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to take responsibility for him." — "They make a living that way, esteemed sir, they make a living that way…" [21 — "They make a living that way…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg's poor people who made a living by deliberately running under carriages to receive compensation for injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and angrily watched the carriage disappearing, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.
