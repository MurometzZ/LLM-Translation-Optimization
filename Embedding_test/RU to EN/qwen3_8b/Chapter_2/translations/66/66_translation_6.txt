The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a restless and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked into the distance for a long and intense time; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to the university, it usually happened—more often than not, when returning home—that he would stop precisely at this same spot, gaze intently at this truly magnificent panorama, and each time almost marvel at one unclear and unsolvable impression of his own. An inexplicable coldness always seemed to blow upon him from this magnificent panorama; this rich picture was full of a silent and heavy spirit… He was always fascinated by his gloomy and enigmatic impression and postponed solving it, doubting himself, to the future. Now suddenly, he sharply recalled these former questions and uncertainties, and it seemed to him that he had not accidentally remembered them now. It seemed strange and strange to him that he had stopped at the same spot again, as before, as if he had indeed imagined that he could think about the same things now as before, and be interested in the same themes and pictures as before… just so recently. It almost seemed funny to him at the same time, and yet it pressed painfully on his chest. In some depth, below, somewhere barely visible under his feet, it seemed to him now all this former past, and former thoughts, and former tasks, and former themes, and former impressions, and the entire panorama, and himself, and everything, everything… It seemed as if he was flying away somewhere upward, and everything was vanishing from his eyes… Making an involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt the double-rouble coin clenched in his fist. He unclenched his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung his arm, and threw it into the water; then he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had cut himself off from all and everything with scissors at this moment.
