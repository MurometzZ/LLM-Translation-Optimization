But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolayevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. A coachman of one carriage struck him hard with a whip on his back for almost running over his horse, despite the coachman having shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he was walking in the very middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), growled fiercely, and gnashed his teeth. Laughter echoed around him. — "To work!" — someone said. "Some drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels. You'll have to take responsibility for him." — "They make a living that way, esteemed sir, they make a living that way…" [21 — "They make a living that way…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg's poor people who made a living by deliberately running under carriage wheels to receive compensation for injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still senselessly and maliciously watching the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.
