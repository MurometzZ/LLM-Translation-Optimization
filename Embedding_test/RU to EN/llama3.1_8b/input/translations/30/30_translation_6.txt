— Ilya Petrovich! — began the clerk carefully, but stopped to wait for a moment, because the boiling officer could not be restrained otherwise than by holding him by the hands, which he knew from his own experience. As for the lush lady, at first she trembled so much from thunder and lightning; but it's a strange thing: the more numerous and fierce the insults became, the more charming her appearance became, the more enchanting her smile turned out to be, directed at the stern officer. She was hopping on one foot and constantly squatting, impatiently waiting for them finally to allow her to speak up, and she waited. — There will be no noise or fight here with me, Mr. Captain, — suddenly babbled she, as if they had poured out a handful of peas, with a strong German accent, although speaking quickly in Russian, — and there won't be any scandal, not at all, but they came drunk, and I'll tell you everything, Mr. Captain, and I'm not to blame... I have a noble house, Mr. Captain, and noble manners, Mr. Captain, and I always, always didn't want any scandal. And they completely came drunk and then asked three times for the bill, and then one raised his legs and started playing the piano with his feet, and that's not nice at all in a noble house, and he broke the piano, and there is absolutely no manner here, and I said so. And he took the pipe and started pushing everyone behind him with it. And then as soon as I quickly called the janitor to call Karl, he took Karl and poked his eye, and Henriette too poked her eye, and they hit me five times on the cheek. And that's not nice at all in a noble house, Mr. Captain, and I shouted. And he opened the window onto the canal and stood in the window like a little pig, squealing; and that's shameful.
