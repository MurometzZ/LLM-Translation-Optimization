—"A cup of tea?" — "I'll have a cup of tea." — "Pour it." — "Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit at the table." He immediately arranged, poured, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back on the sofa. He continued to hold the sick man's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began feeding him with a teaspoon, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the very act of blowing were the main and saving point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite enough strength within himself to lift up and sit on the sofa without any outside help, and not only to hold the spoon or cup in his hands, but even perhaps to walk. But by some strange, almost beast-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even though he didn't quite understand yet, but at the same time to listen and find out what was going on here? In any case, he could not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed away the spoon, and collapsed again onto the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows under his head—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.
