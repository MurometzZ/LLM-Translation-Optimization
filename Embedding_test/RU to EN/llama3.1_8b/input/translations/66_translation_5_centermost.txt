The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; one anxious and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked far off for a long time, intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to university, it often happened that, returning home, he would stop at exactly this same spot, gaze intently at this indeed magnificent panorama, and almost every time be amazed by one obscure and insoluble impression of his own. An inexplicable chill always blew on him from this magnificent panorama; the spirit was mute and deaf to him in this lush picture... He marveled each time at his gloomy and enigmatic impression and put off solving it, not trusting himself, for the future. Suddenly he remembered now these old questions and perplexities of his, and it seemed to him that he had not forgotten them by chance. It struck him as strange and wonderful that he had stopped at exactly the same spot as before, as if he really imagined that he could think about the very same things now as before, and be interested in the same old themes and pictures as he was... just a little while ago. Even it almost seemed funny to him, and at the same time his chest felt constricted with pain. In some depth, below, somewhere barely visible underfoot, it seemed to him that all this past had come alive now, and all these old thoughts, and old problems, and old themes, and old impressions, and the whole panorama, and he himself, and everything... It seemed as if he was flying upwards somewhere, and everything disappeared from his eyes. Making one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a double ruble coin clenched in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung it back and forth, and then threw it into the water; after that, he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had cut himself off from everything and everyone with scissors just now.
