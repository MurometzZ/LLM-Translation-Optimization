But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully looked after the departing wheelchair, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shove money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman in a headscarf and goat-skin boots, and with her a young girl in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. Two grivnas. By their dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real street collector, and for the whole two grivnas he, probably, deserved a whip, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two grivnas in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which rarely happens on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which can be seen best from this spot, from the bridge, twenty paces away from the chapel, was shining, and through the clear air one could clearly see even every ornament of it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the blow; a restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively.
