Louisa Ivanovna with a benevolent smile began to squat down on all sides and, squatting, reached the doors; but in the doorway she bumped her backside into one of the prominent officers, with an open fresh face and magnificent thick light blond whiskers. This was Nikodim Fomich, the quarter's overseer. Louisa Ivanovna hastened to sit down almost to the floor and, jumping up on tiptoes, flew out of the office. — Again thunder, again storm and lightning, tornado, hurricane! — said Nikodim Fomich kindly and affably to Ilya Petrovich, — again they've disturbed my heart, again it's boiling over! I even heard it from the stairs. — What? — said Ilya Petrovich with noble nonchalance (and not what, but somehow: "Da-a sh-ta-a!"), moving with some papers to another table and dramatically flinging his shoulders with each step; wherever he stepped, there was a shoulder; — look here, you see: the gentleman writer, that is, the student, who used to be, doesn't pay money, issued IOUs, doesn't clean up the apartment, constant complaints come in about them, and they dared to enter a claim, saying I lit a cigarette among them! They're whining, but look here at them: look at them in their most attractive state now. — Poverty is not a vice, friend, well, what can you do?
