Further in the courtyard, peering out from behind a fence was the corner of a low, soot-stained stone shed, obviously part of some workshop or other. Here, no doubt, there had been some sort of establishment, a carriage repair shop or a locksmith's, or something similar; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust was scattered about. "If only I could just toss it somewhere and be gone!" he thought suddenly. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he strode through the gates and just caught sight, right by the gates, of a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, cab drivers, etc.) attached to the fence; above the gutter, right on the fence, was written with chalk the usual sharp remark: "Here trespassing is forbidden." So it's all the better that he didn't arouse any suspicion by going and stopping. "Everything here is so convenient - just toss it somewhere in a heap and be gone!"

Glancing around once more, he had already stuffed his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where all the space was only about an arshin wide, he noticed a large uncut stone, weighing roughly one and a half poods, lying flat against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk; you could hear people hurrying by - there are always plenty of them here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone from the street happened to come in, which, however, was quite possible, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed hold of its top with both hands firmly, gathered all his strength and turned the stone over. Underneath the stone a small depression formed; immediately he began throwing everything out of his pocket into it.
