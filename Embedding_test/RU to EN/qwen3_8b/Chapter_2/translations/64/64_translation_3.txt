But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to himself again due to a very unpleasant incident for him. The coachman of one carriage whipped him hard on the back, for not quite getting hit by the horses, despite the coachman having yelled at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he, leaping to the railing (it was unknown why he was walking in the very center of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), snarled and gnashed his teeth. Laughter rang around him, of course. — "And to work!" — "Some drunkard." — "It's known, drunkards will present themselves and intentionally run under the wheels; and you have to take responsibility for them." — "That's their trade, sir, that's their trade…" [21 — "That's their trade…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about the Petersburg poor who made a living by deliberately running under carriages to receive assistance for injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and maliciously looked after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was putting money into his hands.
