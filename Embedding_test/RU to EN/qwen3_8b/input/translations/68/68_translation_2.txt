And lo, to his greatest astonishment, he suddenly recognized the voice of his mistress. She was screaming, wailing, and lamenting, rushing, hurrying, spilling out words so that one could not make out what she was saying, pleading—obviously, to stop beating her, because she was being mercilessly beaten on the stairs. The voice of the abuser had become so terrifying from hatred and fury that it was barely more than a rasp, yet the abuser was also speaking something, incoherently, urgently, stumbling over words. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf: he recognized this voice; it was Ilyi Petrovich. Ilyi Petrovich was here, beating his mistress! He was hitting her with his feet, knocking her head against the steps—this is clear, one can hear it by the sounds, the screams, the blows! What is this, has the world turned upside down? One could hear how crowds were gathering on all the floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, doors slamming, people converging. "But why, why, and how can this be!"—he kept repeating, seriously thinking that he had completely lost his mind. But no, he was hearing it too clearly...
