And suddenly, to his greatest amazement, he caught a glimpse of the voice of his landlady. She was wailing, screaming and lamenting, hurrying, rushing, uttering words so indistinct that they couldn't be made out, begging for something - certainly for them to stop beating her, because she was being mercilessly beaten on the stairs. The voice of the beater had become so terrifying from malice and fury that it could hardly be heard as anything more than a croak, but the beater himself was saying something too, and just as quickly, incoherently, rushing and choking. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf: he recognized this voice; it was Ilya Petrovich's voice! And Ilya Petrovich is here beating his landlady! He's kicking her with his feet, pounding her head against the step - that's clear, that can be heard from the sounds, the screams, the blows! What's going on? Has the light turned upside down? It was audible how a crowd gathered in all the apartments, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, rising, knocking, banging doors, rushing out. "But why, why, and how can this be?" he repeated to himself, seriously thinking that he had gone mad. But no, he's hearing it too clearly!
