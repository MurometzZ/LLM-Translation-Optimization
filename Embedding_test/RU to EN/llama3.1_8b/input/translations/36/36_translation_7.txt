—"Please have mercy, Captain," he began quite freely, addressing Nikodim Fomich suddenly, "take my situation into account... I'm even willing to ask them for forgiveness if I've made any mistakes on my part. I'm a poor and sick student, depressed (he said it himself: 'depressed') by poverty. I used to be a student, but now I can no longer support myself, but I'll get the money... My mother and sister are in the — province. They'll send me some, and I... will pay. My landlady is a kind woman, but she's so angry that I've lost my lessons and haven't paid for four months, not even to eat... And I don't understand at all what this promissory note is! Now she's demanding payment from me on the basis of this loan letter, what am I supposed to do?!" —"But it's not our business..." was again noted by the clerk...

—"Wait a minute, wait a minute," Rascolnikov picked up again, addressing Nikodim Fomich, but trying to address both him and Ilia Petrovich as well, although the latter stubbornly pretended to be rummaging through papers and disdainfully ignored him, "allow me to explain from my side that I've been living with her for about three years now, since I arrived in the province and before... before... anyway, why shouldn't I confess it right away? From the very beginning, I gave her a verbal promise that I would marry her daughter, a completely free promise... She was a girl... well, she even pleased me... although I wasn't in love with her... in short, youth, that is to say, my landlady was then giving me a lot of credit and I was living a rather carefree life... I was very reckless..."

—"We don't need such intimate details from you, sir," Ilia Petrovich interrupted roughly and triumphantly, but Rascolnikov stopped him with passion, although it suddenly became extremely difficult for him to speak. —"But please, let me tell you a bit of what happened... how things were... in my turn... even though it's unnecessary, I agree with you that I should be telling this story..."
