But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully looked after the receding wheelchair, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was pushing money into his hands. He looked around: an old widow, in a headpiece and wooden clogs, and with her a young girl, in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. Two kopecks. By their dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real collector of alms on the street, and for the act of giving the whole two kopecks, he probably deserved a whip, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two kopecks in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, towards the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is rarely seen on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be seen better from any point than from here, from the bridge, twenty steps away from the chapel, gleamed, and through the clear air, one could clearly see even each of its ornaments. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a single restless and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively.
