This was some particularly disheveled person with a fixed idea in his gaze. "You won't learn anything from this, because it's all the same to him," thought Raskolnikov. "Go there, to the scribe," said the clerk, and with a finger he pushed forward, indicating the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), a cramped and densely packed space with people—more decently dressed than in those rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One, in mourning, poorly dressed, sat at the table opposite the scribe and was writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and ruddy, with freckles, an obvious woman, dressed in an extremely elaborate manner, with a brooch on her chest as large as a tea saucer, stood aside and was waiting for something. Raskolnikov slipped his summons to the scribe. The scribe glanced at it briefly, said, "Wait," and continued working with the mourning lady. He exhaled more freely.
