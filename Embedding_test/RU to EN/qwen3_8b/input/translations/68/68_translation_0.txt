And lo and behold, to his greatest astonishment, he suddenly recognized the voice of his mistress. She was screaming, wailing, and crying out, rushing and hurrying, spewing words so fast that they couldn't be understood, pleading—obviously, begging them to stop beating her, because she was being mercilessly beaten on the staircase. The attacker's voice had become so terrifying from hatred and fury that it was barely more than a rasp, yet the attacker was still uttering something incoherent, speaking hurriedly, indistinctly, struggling to get words out. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf—he recognized this voice; it was Ilyi Petrovich. Ilyi Petrovich was here, beating his mistress! He was hitting her with his feet, knocking her head against the steps—this was clear, audible from the sounds, the screams, the blows! What was this? Had the world turned upside down? One could hear crowds gathering on all the floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, doors slamming, people gathering. "But for what, for what, and how could this be!"—he kept repeating, seriously thinking that he had completely lost his mind. But no, he was hearing it all too clearly...
