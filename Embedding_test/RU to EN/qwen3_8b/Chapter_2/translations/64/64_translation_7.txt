But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolayevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. A coachman of one of the sleighs whipped him hard on the back for almost running over his horse, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he had been walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), and he gnashed and chattered his teeth in fury. Laughter echoed around him. — "To work!" — someone said. — "Somehow, a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; but you have to take responsibility for him." — "That's how they make a living, esteemed sir, that's how they make a living…" [21 — "That's how they make a living…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg beggars who made a living by deliberately running under carriages to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and angrily watched the sleigh disappearing, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.
