Next, in the recess of the courtyard, a low, soot-stained stone shed peeped out from behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, probably, was some kind of establishment, a carriage workshop or a blacksmith's shop, or something of the sort; everywhere, almost from the very gates, soot dust was visible. "That would be a perfect place to toss something and disappear!" he suddenly thought. Noticing no one in the courtyard, he stepped through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, carriage drivers, etc.), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark: "Here is the place where the cart is forbidden." That was good, then, since there was no suspicion that he had come in and stopped. "Everything could be thrown down here at once and then disappear!" 

Looking around once more, he had already stuck his hand into his pocket when, suddenly, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was a span wide, he noticed a large, uncut stone, about perhaps a pound and a half in weight, lying directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and one could hear pedestrians scurrying about, which there were always quite a few; but no one could see him from behind the gates, unless someone came from the street, which, in fact, could very well happen, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed the top of it firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small depression formed under the stone; immediately he began throwing everything from his pocket into it.
