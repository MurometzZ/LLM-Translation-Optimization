He remembered only taking a swig of cold water and spilling it from the cup onto his chest. Then came the confusion. III

However, he was not entirely in a state of confusion throughout the illness; it was a feverish state, with delirium and semi-consciousness. He later remembered much. At times it seemed to him that a crowd was gathering around him, wanting to take him somewhere, and they were arguing and quarreling about him. At other times, he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone had left, and they feared him, and only occasionally slightly opened the door to look at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves about something, laughing, and taunting him. He often remembered Natasha by his side; he distinguished another person, who seemed very familiar to him, but who exactly—could not figure out, and he longed for it, even crying. At times it seemed to him that he had already been lying there for a month; at other times—that it was still the same day. But about that—he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something that could not be forgotten—tormented, suffered, recalling it, groaning, descending into madness or an unbearable fear. Then he would try to get up, wanted to run away, but always someone would stop him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and confusion.
