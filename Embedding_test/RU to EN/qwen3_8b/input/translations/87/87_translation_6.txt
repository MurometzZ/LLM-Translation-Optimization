—"A cup of tea?" — "I'll have a cup of tea." — "Pour it." — "Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit at the table." He immediately arranged, poured, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back on the sofa. He still held the sick man's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began feeding him with a teaspoon, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the act of blowing on the spoon was the main and saving point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite enough strength within himself to lift up and sit on the sofa without any outside help, and not only to control his hands enough to hold the spoon or cup, but even perhaps to walk. But for some strange, almost animal-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even if he didn't quite understand yet, but at the same time to listen and find out what was going on here? In any case, he could not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed away the spoon, and collapsed again on the pillow. Under his head, there were now real pillows—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he also noticed this and took it into consideration.
