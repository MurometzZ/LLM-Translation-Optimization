— Well, isn't she my landlady? — Well, so what if she's the landlady? The clerk looked at him with a compassionate, condescending smile, mixed with a touch of triumph, as if he were a new recruit just beginning to be fired upon: "So, how do you feel now?" But what did he care now about borrowed writing, about the reprimand! Was it worth even the slightest worry, let alone any attention at all! He stood, read, listened, answered, even asked questions himself, but all this was mechanical. The triumph of self-preservation, salvation from the pressing danger—this filled his entire being at that moment, without forethought, without analysis, without future riddles and guesses, without doubts and without questions. This was a moment of complete, immediate, purely animal joy. But at that very moment in the office, something like thunder and lightning occurred. The ensign, still shaken by the disrespect, burning with anger, obviously wanting to support the wounded ambition, launched himself at the unfortunate "puffy lady," who had been looking at him with the most foolish smile since he entered. — And you, such a silly and peculiar one, — he shouted at the top of his lungs (the widow had already left), — what happened to you last night?
