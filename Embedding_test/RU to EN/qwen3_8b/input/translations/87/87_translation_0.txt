—"A cup of tea?" — "I'll have a cup of tea." — "Pour it." — "Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table." He immediately ordered, poured, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back on the couch. He still held the sick man's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began feeding him with a teaspoon, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the very act of blowing were the main and saving point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite strong enough to lift himself up and sit on the couch without any help, and not only to hold the spoon or cup in his hands, but even perhaps to walk. But for some strange, almost beast-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even as if he didn't quite understand yet, but at the same time to listen and find out what was going on here? In any case, he could not overcome his revulsion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, petulantly pushed away the spoon, and collapsed again onto the pillow. Under his head, indeed, there were now real pillows—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.
