Further, in the recess of the courtyard, a corner of a low, soot-stained, stone shed peeped out from behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, probably, was some sort of establishment, a cartwright's or a blacksmith's, or something of the sort; everywhere, almost from the very gates, there was a blackened layer of soot. "That would be a good place to throw something away and disappear!" he suddenly thought. Noticing no one in the courtyard, he stepped through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, coachmen, and so on), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark in such cases: "Here, the cart is forbidden." That was good, then, that there was no suspicion that he had come in and stopped. "Everything could be thrown away all at once somewhere in a heap and then leave!" 

Looking around once more, he had already stuck his hand into his pocket when, suddenly, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was the width of a cubit, he noticed a large, unshaped stone, about one and a half pud in weight, lying directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and one could hear passersby scurrying about, which there were always quite a few; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone came from the street, which, however, could very well happen, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small depression formed under the stone; immediately he began throwing everything from his pocket into it.
