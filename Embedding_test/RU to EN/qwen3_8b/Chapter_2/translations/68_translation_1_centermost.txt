And lo, to his greatest astonishment, he suddenly recognized the voice of his mistress. She was wailing, screaming, and crying out, rushing, hastening, spilling out words so fast that it was impossible to make sense of what she was saying, pleading—obviously, begging them to stop beating her, because she was being mercilessly beaten on the stairs. The voice of the attacker had become so terrifying from hatred and fury that it was barely more than a rasp, yet the attacker himself was also speaking something, incoherently, urgently, stumbling over words, gasping. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf—he recognized this voice; it was Ilyi Petrovich. Ilyi Petrovich was here, beating his mistress! He was kicking her with his feet, knocking her head against the steps—this is clear, one can hear it by the sounds, the screams, the blows! What is this? Has the world turned upside down? One could hear people gathering on all floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, doors slamming, people converging. "But why, why, and how can this be!"—he kept repeating, seriously thinking that he had completely lost his mind. But no, he was hearing it all too clearly...
