From the lips of the unhappy and beloved girl, Raskolnikov, to his own surprise, hears words at first incomprehensible to him, yet irresistible in their inner truth, about how the morality that had so impressed him before—the morality of "extraordinary people," who calmly and without remorse build their calculations on the blood of others—ultimately differs in no way from the inhuman morality openly proclaimed and daily practiced by people of the ruling classes, who have elevated violence and crime into a daily, "normal" law of life. In Raskolnikov's eyes, the true bearers of beauty and morality are now not those who place themselves above ordinary, common people, but rather those, like Sonia, who amidst hunger and humiliation retain in their souls faith in life and humanity, moral steadfastness and unshakable resolve, profound revulsion toward violence, evil, and crime in any form.
