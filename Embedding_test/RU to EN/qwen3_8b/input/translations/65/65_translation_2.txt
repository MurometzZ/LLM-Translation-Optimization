But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully looked after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shove money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman in a headpiece and goat shoes, and with her a girl in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. Two kopecks. By their dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real money-collector on the street, and for the whole two kopecks he, probably, deserved a whip, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two kopecks in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which rarely happens on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be outlined better from any point than from here, from the bridge, not reaching twenty steps to the chapel, shone brilliantly, and through the clear air one could distinctly see even every ornament of it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the blow; a restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively.
