—"And a cup of tea?" "A cup of tea, if you like." "Pour it, then. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately gave orders, poured some, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast and sat back on the divan again. Still holding the sick man's head with his left hand, he lifted him up and began to feed him tea from a teaspoon, blowing on the spoon continuously and especially zealously, as if in this process of blowing on the spoon lay the main and most vital point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite enough strength to lift himself up and sit on the divan without any external help, and not only to hold his hands so that he could hold a spoon or cup, but even to walk. But for some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for the time being, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even still not quite understanding, and in the meantime listen and find out what is happening here? However, he could not overcome his aversion: after taking ten spoons of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away and fell back onto the pillow.
