— Yes, she is my mistress, isn't she? — Well, so what if she is your mistress? The scribe looked at him with a compassionate smile tinged with pity, yet also a touch of triumph, as if he were a new recruit just beginning to be fired upon: "So, what do you feel now, my dear? How do you feel?" But what did it matter now to him about borrowed writing, about the collection! Was it worth even a moment's worry, let alone any attention! He stood, read, listened, answered, even asked questions, but all this was mechanical. The triumph of self-preservation, salvation from the crushing danger—this filled his entire being at that moment, without forethought, without analysis, without future speculation or riddles, without doubts or questions. It was a moment of complete, immediate, purely animal joy. But at that very moment, in the office, something like thunder and lightning occurred. The lieutenant, still shaken by the disrespect, burning with anger, and obviously wishing to support the wounded ambition, launched himself at the unfortunate "puffy lady," who had been looking at him since he entered with the most foolish smile. — You, such a one and such another, — he suddenly shouted at the top of his lungs (the mourning lady had already left), — what happened during the past night?
