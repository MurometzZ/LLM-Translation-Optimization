The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; one anxious and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked far off for a long time, intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to university, it often happened that, returning home, he would stop exactly at this same spot, gaze intently at this indeed magnificent panorama, and almost every time be amazed by one obscure and insoluble impression of his own. An inexplicable chill always blew on him from this magnificent panorama; the spirit was mute and deaf to him in this lush picture... He marveled each time at his gloomy and enigmatic impression and put off solving it, not trusting himself, for the future. Suddenly he remembered now all these former questions and perplexities of his own, and it seemed to him that he had not forgotten them by chance. It even struck him as strange and wonderful that he had stopped in exactly the same place as before, as if he really believed that he could think about the very same things now as before, with the same old themes and pictures that interested him... still so recently. Even a little bit funny it seemed to him, and at the same time his chest felt constricted to the point of pain. In some depth, below, somewhere just visible underfoot, it seemed to him now that all this former past was, and former thoughts, and former problems, and former themes, and former impressions, and the whole panorama, and he himself, and everything... It seemed as if he were flying upwards somewhere and everything was disappearing from his eyes. Having made one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a double ruble coin clenched in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung it back and forth, and then threw it into the water; after that, he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had cut himself off from everything and everyone with scissors just now.
