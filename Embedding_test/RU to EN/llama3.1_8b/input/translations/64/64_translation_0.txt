But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolsky Bridge, he was forced to come to his senses once again due to a rather unpleasant incident for him. A coachman from one of the carriages gave him a tight lash on the back with his whip because he almost got hit by the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had yelled at him three or four times. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that he, jumping away to the railing (he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where vehicles pass, not pedestrians), snarled and snapped his teeth in anger. Of course, laughter echoed all around. — And what's the matter? — Some kind of beggar. — It's well known that a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; and you're responsible for him. — They make their living that way, sir. — They make their living that way...[21 - They make their living that way… — In newspapers from that time, it was often reported about St. Petersburg beggars who made their living by intentionally running under the carriage wheels to receive compensation for their injuries (see, e.g., "Golos", 1865, No. 65).] But at the moment when he stood by the railing and still looked after the departing carriage with a senseless and angry expression, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was slipping money into his hands.
