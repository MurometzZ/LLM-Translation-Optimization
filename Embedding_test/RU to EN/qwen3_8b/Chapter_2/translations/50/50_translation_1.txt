Next, in the recess of the courtyard, a low, soot-stained stone shed protruded from behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, probably, was some sort of establishment, a carriage workshop or a blacksmith's, or something of the sort; everywhere, almost from the very gates, there was a thick layer of soot. "That's where you could toss something and disappear!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he stepped through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, carriage drivers, etc.), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark: "Here, carts are prohibited." That was good, then, that there was no suspicion that he had come in and stopped. "Everything could be thrown down here at once and then disappear!" 

Looking around once more, he had already stuck his hand into his pocket when, suddenly, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was about a sazhene wide, he noticed a large, unpolished stone, roughly about one and a half pud (approximately 1.5 kg) in weight, lying directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and one could hear pedestrians scurrying about, which there were always quite a few; but no one could see him from behind the gates, unless someone came from the street, which, in fact, could very well happen, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small depression formed under the stone; immediately, he began throwing everything from his pocket into it.
