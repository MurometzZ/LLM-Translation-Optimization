The plump lady jumped up from her seat at the sight of him and with a peculiar excitement began to squat; but the officer paid no attention to her whatsoever, and she was no longer able to sit next to him. He was a lieutenant, an assistant to the quarter-master sergeant, with horizontally sprouting reddish sideburns and extremely small facial features that expressed nothing in particular except for some haughtiness. He glanced at Raskolnikov from the corner of his eye and partly with indignation: Raskolnikov's outfit was too shabby, and despite all the humiliation, he still didn't have a proper posture; Raskolnikov, through carelessness, looked straight at him for too long, so that the other even took offense. — What do you want? — he shouted, probably surprised that such a ragamuffin did not cower from his lightning-fast glance. — They demanded... by summons... — Raskolnikov replied somehow awkwardly. — This is about collecting money from them, from the student, — the clerk hastened to explain, tearing himself away from the papers. — Here! — and he handed Raskolnikov a notebook, pointing out the place in it, — read!
