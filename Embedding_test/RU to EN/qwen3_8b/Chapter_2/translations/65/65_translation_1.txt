But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully watched the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone pushing money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman who sold goods, in a headscarf and goat-skin boots, and with her a young girl, in a hat and with a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. A two-kopeck coin. By their dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real street beggar, and for the whole two-kopeck coin he, probably, deserved a knout, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two-kopeck coin in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is so rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which can be seen best from this spot, from the bridge, twenty paces away from the chapel, gleamed, and through the clear air one could clearly see even every ornament of it. The pain from the knout had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the blow; a restless and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively.
