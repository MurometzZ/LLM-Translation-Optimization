But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolayevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. The driver of a carriage whipped him hard on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite the driver having shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), gnashing and chattering his teeth in fury. Laughter echoed around him. — "To work!" — someone said. "Somehow, a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to answer for him." — "That's their trade, sir, that's their trade…" [21 — "That's their trade…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg beggars who deliberately ran under carriages to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still senselessly and maliciously watching the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.
