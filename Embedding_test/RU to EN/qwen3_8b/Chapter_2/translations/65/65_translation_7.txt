But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully watched the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shove money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman in a headscarf and goat-skin boots, and with her a young girl in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. A two-kopeck coin. By his dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real street collector of alms, and for the handing over of a whole two-kopeck coin, he probably deserved a whip, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two-kopeck coin in his hand, walked ten paces, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which rarely happens on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be seen better from any point than from here, from the bridge, not yet twenty paces from the chapel, shone brilliantly, and through the clear air, one could clearly discern even every ornament of it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the blow; a restless and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively.
