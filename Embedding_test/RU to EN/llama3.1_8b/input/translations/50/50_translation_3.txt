Further in the courtyard, peering out from behind a fence was the corner of a low, soot-stained stone shed, obviously part of some workshop or other. Here, no doubt, there had been some kind of establishment, a carriage shop or a locksmith's, or something similar; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust was scattered about. "Just think where I could toss it and be gone!" he suddenly thought to himself. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he strode through the gates and just caught sight, right by the gates, of a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses with many factory workers, artisans, cabmen, etc.) attached to the fence; and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharpness: "Here trespassing is forbidden." So it's all the better that there was no suspicion of him having gone in and stopped. "Everything here is so neatly arranged - just toss it somewhere into a heap and be off!"

Glancing around once more, he had already stuffed his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where all the space was only as wide as an arshin, he noticed a large uncut stone, weighing about one and a half poods, lying flat against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk; you could hear passersby shuffling along - there were always plenty of them here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone from the street happened to come in, which, however, was quite possible and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed hold of its top with both hands firmly, gathered all his strength and turned the stone over. Underneath the stone a small depression formed; immediately he began throwing everything out of his pocket into it.
