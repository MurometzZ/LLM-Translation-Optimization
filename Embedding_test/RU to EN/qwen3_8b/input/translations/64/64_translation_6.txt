But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolayevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. A coachman of one of the sleighs had whipped him hard on the back for almost running over his horse, despite the coachman having shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he had been walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), growled maliciously, and gnashed his teeth. Laughter echoed around him. — "To work!" — someone said. "Some drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to answer for him." — "They make a living from it, esteemed sir, they make a living from it..." [21 — "They make a living from it..." — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg's poor people who made a living by deliberately running under the wheels of carriages in order to receive compensation for injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still senselessly and maliciously watching the departing sleigh, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slipping money into his hands.
