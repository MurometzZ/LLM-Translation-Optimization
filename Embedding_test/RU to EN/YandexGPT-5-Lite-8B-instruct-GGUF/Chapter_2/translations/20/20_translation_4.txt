 It was some particularly disheveled man with a fixed idea in his gaze. "You won't find out anything from this because he doesn't care," Raskolnikov thought. "Go there, to the clerk," said the scribe and pointed forward with his finger, indicating the very last room. He entered that room (the fourth in order), cramped and packed with people — folk a bit cleaner-dressed than those in the other rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One in mourning, poorly dressed, was sitting at the table opposite the clerk and writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very stout and ruddy-faced, spotted, a noticeable woman, and overly elaborately dressed with a brooch on her chest as big as a tea saucer, was standing off to one side waiting for something. Raskolnikov handed the clerk his summons. The clerk glanced at it briefly, said "wait" and continued dealing with the lady in mourning. He breathed more freely.
