—"A cup of tea?" — "I'll have a cup of tea." — "Pour it." — "Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit at the table." He immediately arranged, poured, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back on the couch. He continued to hold the sick man's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began feeding him with a teaspoon, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the very act of blowing were the main and saving point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite strong enough to lift himself up and sit on the couch without any outside help, and not only to hold the spoon or cup, but perhaps even to walk. But for some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even as if he didn't quite understand yet, while at the same time listening and finding out what was going on. Nevertheless, he could not overcome his revulsion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away, and collapsed back onto the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows under his head—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.
