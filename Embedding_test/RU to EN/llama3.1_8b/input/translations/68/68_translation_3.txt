And suddenly, to his greatest amazement, he caught a glimpse of the voice of his landlady. She was wailing, screaming and sobbing, rushing, hurrying, uttering words so indistinct that they could not be made out, begging for something - certainly for them to stop beating her, because she was being mercilessly beaten on the stairs. The voice of the beater had become so terrifying from malice and fury that it was almost a mere croak, but still the beater too was saying something, and just as quickly, incoherently, rushing and choking. Suddenly Raskolnikov quivered like a leaf: he recognized this voice; it was the voice of Ilya Petrovich. Ilya Petrovich is here and beating his landlady! He's kicking her with his feet, pounding her head against the step - that's clear from the sounds, the screams, the blows! What's going on? Has the light turned upside down? It could be heard how a crowd was gathering in all the apartments, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations were rising, knocking, banging on doors, rushing. "But why, why, and how can this be?" he repeated to himself, seriously thinking that he had gone completely mad. But no, he is hearing it all too clearly!
