Further in the courtyard, peering out from behind a fence was the corner of a low, soot-stained stone shed, obviously part of some workshop or other. Here, no doubt, there had been some kind of establishment, a carriage repair shop or a locksmith's, or something similar; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust was scattered about. "If only I could toss it all away and be gone!" he suddenly thought to himself. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he strode through the gates and just caught sight of a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, cab drivers, etc.) right by the gates, and above the gutter, on the fence itself, was written in chalk the usual sharp warning: "Here trespassing is forbidden." So it was all the better that he hadn't raised any suspicions. "I could just toss everything here into a heap and be gone!" He looked around once more, already shoving his hand into his pocket, when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where there was a space of about an arshin in width, he noticed a large uncut stone, weighing roughly one and a half poods, lying flat against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk; you could hear passersby shuffling along - there were always plenty here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone from the street happened to come in, which, however, was quite possible, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed hold of its top with both hands, gathered all his strength, and turned the stone over. Underneath it a small depression formed; immediately he began throwing everything from his pocket into it.
