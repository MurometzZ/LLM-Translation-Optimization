But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolayevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. The driver of a carriage struck him firmly with a whip on his back for almost hitting the horses, despite the driver having shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), screeched maliciously, and clamped his teeth together. Laughter echoed around him. — "To work!" — someone said. "Somehow, a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to answer for him." — "They make a living by that, esteemed sir; they make a living by that…" [21 — "They make a living by that…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg poor people who made a living by deliberately running under carriages to receive compensation for injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and maliciously stared after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.
