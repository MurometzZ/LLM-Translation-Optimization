But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. The coachman of one of the sleighs whipped him hard on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), growled fiercely, and clamped his teeth together. Laughter echoed around him. — "To work!" — someone said. "Somehow, a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to take responsibility for him." — "That's how they make a living, esteemed sir, that's how they make a living…" [21 — "That's how they make a living…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about the Petersburg poor who made a living by deliberately running under the wheels of a carriage to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still senselessly and maliciously watching the departing sleigh, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.
