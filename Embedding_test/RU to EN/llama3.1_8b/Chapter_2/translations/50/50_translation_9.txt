Further in the courtyard, peering out from behind a fence was the corner of a low, blackened stone shed, obviously part of some workshop or other. Here, no doubt, there had been some kind of establishment, a carriage shop or a locksmith's, or something similar; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust was scattered about. "If only I could just toss it somewhere and be gone!" he suddenly thought to himself. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he strode through the gates and just caught sight of a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, cabmen, etc.) right next to the gates, and above the gutter, on the fence itself, was written with chalk the usual sharp phrase: "Here trespassing is forbidden." So it's all for the best that he didn't arouse any suspicion by going in and stopping. "Everything here is so conveniently arranged - just toss it somewhere and be gone!"

Glancing around once more, he had already stuffed his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where all the space was only about an arshin wide, he noticed a large uncut stone, weighing roughly one and a half poods, lying flat against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk; you could hear people hurrying by - there were always plenty of them here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone from the street happened to come in, which, however, was quite possible, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grasped its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and turned the stone over. Underneath it a small depression formed; immediately he began throwing everything out of his pocket into it.
