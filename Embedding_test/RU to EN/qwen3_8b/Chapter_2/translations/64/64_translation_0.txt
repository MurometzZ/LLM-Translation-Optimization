But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. A coachman of one of the carriages struck him firmly with a whip on the back for almost stepping on the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge where cars go, not pedestrians), grinded his teeth maliciously and clicked them. Laughter was heard all around. — "To work!" — someone said. "Somehow, a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to answer for him." — "That's their trade, esteemed sir, that's their trade…" [21 — "That's their trade…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg poor people who made a living by deliberately running under carriages' wheels to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and maliciously looked after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.
