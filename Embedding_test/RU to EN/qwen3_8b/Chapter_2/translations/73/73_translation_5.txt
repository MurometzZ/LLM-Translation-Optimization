He remembered only how he had taken a swig of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came confusion. III

He, however, was not entirely in a state of confusion throughout the illness: it was a feverish condition, with delirium and semi-consciousness. Much later he remembered many things. At times it seemed to him that a crowd was gathering around him, wanting to take him somewhere, arguing and quarreling about him. At other times he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone had left, and they feared him, opening the door only occasionally to look at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves, laughing, and mocking him. He often remembered Nastasya by his side; he distinguished another person, very much like someone he knew, but who exactly—he could not figure out and pined for it, even crying. At times it seemed to him that he had been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about that he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something he couldn't forget—tormented, suffered, recalling, groaning, descending into fury or an awful, unbearable fear. Then he would try to get up, wanted to run away, but always someone would stop him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and confusion.
