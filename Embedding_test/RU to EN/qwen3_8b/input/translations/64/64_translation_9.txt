But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to once again fully come to his senses due to a very unpleasant incident. A coachman of a carriage had whipped him hard on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite the coachman shouting at him several times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railings (it was unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge where carriages go, not pedestrians), and he gnashed his teeth angrily. Laughter echoed around him, of course. — "And let's get to work!" — someone said. "Some drunkard is going to deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to take responsibility for him." — "That's their trade, esteemed sir, that's their trade…" [21 — "That's their trade…" — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg beggars who deliberately ran under carriages to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railings, still mindlessly and angrily watching the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone putting money into his hands.
